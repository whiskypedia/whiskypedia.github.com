On The Rocks

It can happen at a bar, or at a party at someone else's house. You decide on a glass of whisky, and inevitably they ask if you'd like rocks. If your drink is a single malt Scotch whisky the proper answer is "no, thank you".

It's not that ice is always a poor choice. There are whiskies that are better with rocks. The extra chill and dilution work with harsher whiskies with fewer delicate flavours. Most Irish whiskies, Canadian rye whiskies, and American bourbons benefit from ice. The odd blended Scotch whisky will also benefit â€” specifically the ones made with harsher, less aged barrels. With some of these whiskies, ice is the only thing that can save a whisky from being drowned in coke.

But if you're drinking a single malt Scotch whisky (and you certainly should be), keep the ice away from it.

Ice changes a whisky in two ways. It adds a chill which tames the flavour of the drink itself, and it dilutes over time as the cubes melt. Neither of these effects are suitable for single malt whiskies.

The first casualties of the chill are the more subtle and interesting flavours in a single malt. Chilling a single malt will often make it harder to distinguish from a blended whisky. In that case, you may as well save some dollars and pour a glass of Johnny Walker.

Dilution can be a good thing for a single malt, but it has to be limited and controlled. A constantly increasing dilution from melting ice will make the last half of the whisky a watery mess. Watery whisky gets abandoned or chugged, but never savoured. Poor dilution ruins a single malt.

In short, ice is a poor idea in single malts and even single cask bourbon. If you need ice in your single malt, it's probably not the drink for you. On the other hand, for blends and harsher whiskies, ice can make the drink worthwhile.
