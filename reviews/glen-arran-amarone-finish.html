Glen Arran Amarone Review

Steve's Verdict

Amarone is a neat gimmick. Fine to taste once and move on. Save some money and buy the 10 year old instead.

This is another in Glen Arran's line of uniquely finished bottlings. Like the others, it's semi-cask strength at 50%. It's got a bit of a bite, but the finish is strong enough to compete with that extra alcohol.

If you're not into wine, I'll bring you up to speed. Amarone is a full-bodied Italian red wine. It's a favourite with steaks and other big, meaty foods.

The Amarone finish does add some sweetness but not as much as sherry. Sherried malts generally have a long, warm finish, but the Amarone behaves more like a dry wine. It's somewhat tart and then dries up on the palate much quicker than you'd expect. I don't know how to describe it because it's closer to a wine effect than a whisky effect.

It's also impossible to drink this whole line of Glen Arran without getting the impression that it's all gimmickry. It's beautifully executed gimmickry, but that doesn't make it any better. We've faulted malts for using sherry as a crutch to provide all their flavour, and these exotic finishes are no better. It becomes all about the finish. We get worked up because Glen Arran whiskies don't need gimmicks to be world-class single malts.

There's little else to say about this malt once the finish has been covered. The finish makes it a one trick pony. Of course, it's a hell of a trick, and like nothing you're going to get elsewhere. It's not going to taste like any other single malt, so it's worth a try on that basis alone. But it's not going to be a staple for many.

It's hard to describe the weird combination of happiness I have at finding a new Glen Arran bottling, combined with the disappointment that it's one of these oddball bottlings. I can't wait to see what they have in store for us, but I'll be happier when it's just a straight bottling.

medium, finished
